# Sub-prompt A: Deep-Dive + Framework Fill

**Load this sub-prompt for Turn 1 only.** This contains the detailed methodology for the initial analysis pass.

---

## Step 1: Parse & Audit

Read the client materials thoroughly. Extract and organize all available information:

| Category | What to extract |
|----------|----------------|
| Product capabilities | Features, functionality, technology, integrations, form factor |
| Customer context | Who uses it, who buys it, segments, personas, use cases |
| Pain points | Problems solved, triggers, urgency, cost of inaction |
| Current alternatives | What customers use today (competitors, manual processes, spreadsheets, doing nothing) |
| Differentiation claims | What the client says makes them unique |
| Proof points | Metrics, case studies, testimonials, traction data |
| Business model | Pricing, tiers, revenue model |

For each category, assess:

- **Sufficient** — enough data to work with
- **Partial** — some data, will require assumptions (flag them with [H-high/mid/low])
- **Missing** — critical gap, will need Research Order or Decision Card

For every Partial or Missing item, start thinking about what Research Order or Decision Card is needed. These feed directly into Turn 5 output.

---

## Step 2: Product Deep-Dive — "What Is This Business REALLY About?"

**This step is mandatory and happens BEFORE any framework application.** The goal is to develop a genuine understanding of the product — not parrot the client's marketing language, but articulate the deeper transformation this product enables.

**Write the following sections in your own words, NOT by copying client materials:**

### A. Reformulation
Describe the product in your own words as if explaining it to a smart friend who knows nothing about it. Do NOT use the client's taglines, marketing copy, or product description verbatim. If you catch yourself copying their language, stop and rewrite.

### B. Transformation Map
Answer: **What transformation does the customer undergo?**

```
State A (before): [Describe the customer's world without this product — be specific
about what's broken, missing, or painful. Not "they have a problem" but what their
Tuesday looks like.]

→ Transformation: [What specifically changes? What does the product make possible
that wasn't possible before?]

State B (after): [Describe the customer's world after successful adoption.
What's different about their Tuesday now?]
```

### C. The Hard Part
Answer: **What is genuinely hard about what this product does? What can't a competitor replicate in 1-3 months?**

Consider:
- Domain expertise baked into the product (methodology, training programs, proprietary frameworks)
- Data or network effects that compound over time
- Integration depth that creates switching costs
- Combination of software + human expertise that's hard to unbundle
- Category creation — teaching the market a new way to think

If you can't identify something genuinely hard to copy, **say so explicitly**. This is a critical positioning gap.

### D. The "Enough Moment"
Answer: **When does the customer realize they NEED this?** Not "when do they buy" but what event, frustration, or realization triggers the search?

Good examples: "The CEO asks 'why don't we use AI yet?' and nobody has an answer." / "They try ChatGPT for work, it hallucinates, and they realize they need something trained on their own data." / "A competitor ships an AI feature and they panic."

### E. Hidden Value Propositions
Answer: **What value does this product deliver that the client themselves may not articulate?**

Common patterns:
- The product is really selling a **capability upgrade** (from "can't do X" to "can do X"), not a tool
- The product is really selling **risk reduction** (from "terrified of X" to "X is handled"), not features
- The product is really selling **organizational transformation** (from "old way" to "new way"), not software
- The product bundles **education + implementation**, but only talks about the software part

**Output:** a short ~1 page narrative covering sections A-E. This narrative becomes the foundation for ALL subsequent sections. When filling in the framework, constantly reference back to this deep-dive — if your Unique Attributes or Value Map contradict the deep-dive, something is wrong.

**Critical rule:** If the deep-dive reveals that the product's TRUE differentiator is different from what the client emphasizes, note the discrepancy explicitly.

## Step 3: Unique Attributes (Section 3)

> "What do you have that alternatives lack?"

At this stage (Turn 1), you likely don't have the full competitive analysis yet. Work with what you can extract from client materials.

List capabilities, features, and characteristics that the client claims are differentiated. For each:
- Name and brief description
- What it differentiates against (if known from materials — otherwise mark [H])
- Hard differentiator (can't copy in 1-3 months) vs. soft (could copy but haven't)

**Mark every attribute [E] or [H-high/mid/low].** Most will be [H] at this stage — that's fine. They'll be sharpened in Turn 2 when real competitive data arrives.

### Strategic Differentiation Narrative (MANDATORY)

After listing attributes, write a narrative answering:

1. **"Why is this impossible to copy?"** — For top 1-2 differentiators, explain defensibility. Be specific — "our AI is better" is not defensible.
2. **"Why can't the customer go back after trying us?"** — Switching costs: technical, operational, strategic, psychological.
3. **"What does this change in their business?"** — Connect to Deep-Dive Transformation Map.
4. **Moat Analysis** — Classify using: network effects, data accumulation, switching costs, expertise lock-in, process embedding, or none identified.

## Step 4: Value Map (Section 4)

> "What value do those unique attributes deliver?"

For each attribute from Step 3:

```
Attribute → What it enables → Why that matters → Measurable/provable outcome
```

Value types: Operational (saves time), Financial (saves money), Strategic (new capabilities), Emotional (reduces anxiety).

For each value claim, rate confidence:
- **[E] Proven** — has metrics/testimonials from materials
- **[E] Claimed** — client says so in materials
- **[H-high] Inferred** — strong logical connection from materials
- **[H-mid] Inferred** — reasonable connection, some assumptions
- **[H-low] Speculative** — weak signal, needs validation

---

## Step 5: Market Category (Section 6)

> "What frame of reference makes your value obvious?"

Evaluate three positioning styles:

**A. Head-to-Head** — Compete in an existing well-known category. Best when: you can credibly claim leadership or strong differentiation.

**B. Niche/Subsegment** — Dominate a specific segment of an existing category. Best when: you're strong in a specific use case but can't win the whole category.

**C. New Category** — Create and name a new category. Best when: no existing category captures what you do, AND you have the budget/patience to educate the market.

For each viable option: proposed category name, what expectations it sets, what it makes easy/hard to explain, risk assessment.

**Recommend one** with clear reasoning. Mark [H-mid] — this will likely be revised after segments arrive in Turn 3.

---

## Step 6: Relevant Trends (Section 7)

Only include if client materials mention relevant trends. List trends that:
- Make the problem more acute (regulatory changes, market shifts, tech evolution)
- Make the solution more viable (infrastructure readiness, cultural shifts)
- Add urgency to the buying decision

**Critical rule:** Trends are seasoning, not foundation. Never position on a trend alone.

## Step 7: Issue Research Orders

After completing all sections, output structured Research Orders at the end:

### CompetitiveResearchOrder

```
RESEARCH ORDER: Competitive Analysis
FOR: Competitive Analysis Agent

CONTEXT: [1-2 sentences about what the product does, from your Deep-Dive]

WHAT I NEED:
- Full competitive landscape for [product category/space]
- Direct competitors, indirect competitors, manual workarounds, hiring alternatives, "do nothing" option
- For each: what they do, pricing if available, where they fall short

WHAT I ALREADY KNOW FROM CLIENT MATERIALS:
[List any competitors or alternatives mentioned in the materials]

KEY QUESTIONS TO ANSWER:
[2-3 specific questions that emerged from your analysis, e.g., "Is there a dominant player
in [specific niche] that the client didn't mention?"]
```

### SegmentsResearchOrder

```
RESEARCH ORDER: Target Segments
FOR: Target Segments Agent

CONTEXT: [1-2 sentences about product + key value from your Deep-Dive]

WHAT I NEED:
- JTBD-based segment hypotheses
- Scored and prioritized for FL outreach viability
- Concrete enough to find 50 companies per segment in 30 minutes

WHAT I ALREADY KNOW FROM CLIENT MATERIALS:
[List any segments, customer types, or use cases mentioned]

DEEP-DIVE INSIGHTS TO USE:
- Transformation: [1 sentence from Transformation Map]
- Enough Moment: [1 sentence]
- Hidden Value: [1 sentence]
```

### MonetisationResearchOrder (if pricing info is missing/insufficient)

```
RESEARCH ORDER: Monetisation
FOR: Monetisation Agent

CONTEXT: [1-2 sentences about product + current pricing if any]

WHAT I NEED:
- Stage-appropriate pricing model
- Russian market adaptations
- Price anchoring relative to competitive alternatives

WHAT I ALREADY KNOW:
[Any pricing, business model, or revenue info from materials]
```
